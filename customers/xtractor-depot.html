The problem

Four VAs doing the same email, every day, for different reasons.

By 2024, Xtractor Depot was a fast-scaling supplier of precision lab equipment, with customers ranging from regional cannabis labs to multi-billion-dollar manufacturers like SpaceX and Lucid Motors. Order volume was up. Margins were healthy. The team was tired.

The bottleneck wasn't sales. It was operations — specifically the repetitive, semi-structured work that piles up in any growing physical-goods business:

  • Quote intake — emails arriving from across multiple industries, each requiring SKU lookup, stock check, and a personalized response
  • Follow-up emails — chasing customers who'd gone quiet on a quote
  • Status updates — "where's my shipment" pings into a busy inbox
  • Lead qualification — sorting genuine prospects from the noise

The fix Xtractor Depot tried first was the same one most operators try: hire VAs. By Q3 2024, four virtual assistants were on the team. Combined cost: $14,400 / month, fully loaded. Three of them were doing the same email every day, just for different segments of the business.

Andrew — founder and operator — tried what most founders try next: AI tools. Lindy. Zapier AI. Gumloop. Each worked for a weekend. Each broke when the ERP updated an API or the email format changed. None had a maintainer.

That's when he started building the system that became Ramped.

The deployment

Day 1 to Day 27. One workflow at a time.

Ramped's deployment at Xtractor Depot followed the standard 30-day playbook. Three agents went live in production:

Agent 1: Quote Intake Bot — live on day 18

Reads inbound quote emails in Gmail. Cross-references SKU + stock + pricing in the ERP. Drafts a personalized quote response. Posts to a #sales-quotes Slack channel for one-click approval.

Customer asks "where's my order?" via email or Slack. Bot pulls UPS / FedEx tracking, ERP fulfillment status, and warehouse pick-pack timestamps. Replies with the consolidated answer and proactively flags address mismatches when shipping addresses don't match what's on the customer's most recent invoice.

What surprised them

The bot caught errors humans had been missing for years.

The VAs at Xtractor Depot were sharp. They'd been doing the work for months. But manual cross-referencing has a ceiling — and when a customer's shipping address gets updated mid-engagement, or pricing changes mid-quarter, those edge cases are exactly where humans get tired and miss things.

In its first 60 days, Ramped Bot caught:

  • 3 stale shipping addresses — customers had relocated; outdated address still on file
  • 4 SKU mismatches — product code in customer's PO didn't match the current SKU after a Q2 inventory update
  • 2 net-payment-terms violations — customers on Net 60 attempting to place orders while flagged for late payment
  • 2 incorrect tax-zone calculations — new state-tax rules that had been silently applied wrong on three prior shipments

Each one of those, on average, would have cost between $300 and $4,200 to fix downstream — rerouting fees, customer goodwill, refund processing. Net catch value: ~$23,000 in avoided errors over 60 days. [VERIFY this figure with full Q4 financials before publishing]

What's next

Quarter 2: Inventory forecasting + margin analysis.

With the operational tier humming, Xtractor Depot is now scoping the next two agents:

  • Inventory Forecasting Bot — reads sales velocity + supplier lead times + seasonal patterns, surfaces low-stock SKUs to the procurement Slack channel before they hit reorder threshold
  • Margin Analysis Bot — pulls weekly P&L from QuickBooks, flags products whose net margin has dropped below the 22% threshold, drafts a memo for management review

Both will be scoped on the next quarterly strategy call. Estimated time-to-live: 30 days each. Estimated incremental value: hard to quantify, but Andrew's expectation is "another VA's worth of leverage, without another VA."
