# Case Study Template — Ramped AI

A reusable structure for customer case studies. Hits the four things every B2B buyer wants to see: who they are, what hurt, what we did, and what changed (in numbers). Aim for 800-1,200 words per case study.

---

## Title formula

**Pattern:** "[Customer name]: [outcome in numbers] in [timeframe]"

Examples:
- "Cascade Dental Group: Saving 9 hours/week across 4 locations in 21 days"
- "Meridian Field Services: Replacing 3 ops VAs in 30 days"
- "Vantage Property: Cutting first-response time from 14 hours to 11 minutes"

## Section 2 — The hook (first 100 words)

Lead with the punchiest data point. Then humanize.

**Template:**
> "[Customer name] was spending [X hours/week] on [specific manual workflow]. Three weeks after kickoff with Ramped, that number was [Y hours/week] — and the bot was catching errors humans had been missing.
> 
> Here's how the deployment actually went."

Don't start with their company history. Start with the gap that brought them to us.

---

## Section 3 — The problem (200-300 words)

What was broken before Ramped. Be specific and use their language.

**Template:**
> "[Customer name] runs [N locations / scope]. [Their team] was spending [hours/week] on [list of 2-3 specific workflows]. Despite having [VAs / part-time hires / a previous AI tool], the work kept growing because [reason — e.g., compounding customer base, calendar pressures, manual cross-references].
> 
> They'd tried:
> - [Solution 1] — what worked / what didn't
> - [Solution 2] — what worked / what didn't
> - [Solution 3] — what worked / what didn't
> 
> The breaking point came when [specific incident — wrong shipment, missed lead, etc.]. That's when [decision maker] booked a discovery call with Ramped."

> Connects: [systems integrated]
> Triggers on: [event]
> Outputs: [deliverable]
> Approval: [yes/no — if yes, where in the flow]
> 
> **Agent 2: [name]** — [what it does]

> 
> The first agent went live on day [X]. By day 30, all [N] agents were running. Slack screenshot below shows a typical Tuesday morning."

[Include 1-2 redacted Slack screenshots — best visual asset for case studies]

## Section 5 — The numbers (200-300 words)

The before/after table. Bold the headlines.

**Template (table):**

| Metric | Before Ramped | After Ramped | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hours/week on [workflow] | 14 | 5 | **−9 hrs (−64%)** |
| First-response time | 4.2 hrs | 11 min | **−96%** |
| Error rate (wrong shipping addresses, etc.) | ~3/month | 0 in 60 days | **−100%** |
| Monthly cost | $4,200 (3 VAs at $1,400) | $5,000 (Ramped Growth) | +$800/mo |
| Net hours saved (by team) | — | 36 hrs/mo | — |

**Then the meta-takeaway:**
> "Even with a higher monthly spend, the [customer team] freed up [X hours/month] for higher-value work. Within [Y months], [decision maker] redirected the saved budget into [growth investment]."

## Section 6 — The quote (1 paragraph)

The customer's words. Real, specific, attributable.

**Template:**
> "Our [role] used to spend half his Tuesday morning on quote intake. Now Ramped Bot does it in 14 seconds and we catch errors we never used to see. The wild part? We didn't increase ops budget. We just stopped paying VAs to do the same email."
> 
> — [Name], [Title], [Customer Company]

Pull a real quote. Don't manufacture. If you don't have one yet, ASK at the 60-day check-in.

> 
> Long term, the goal is [their words]."

## What makes a GREAT Ramped case study

1. **Numbers people can verify.** "We saved 40 hours" with no method = puff. "We saved 9 hrs/week tracked across these 3 specific workflows" = credible.

2. **Real Slack screenshots.** Even redacted, the visual proves the work happens in the customer's actual environment.

3. **A quote with personality.** "Our director used to spend half his Tuesday morning..." beats "We're so happy with Ramped." The first sounds like a person, not a marketing AI.

4. **The unexpected outcome.** What surprised the customer? "We didn't expect the agent to catch errors we'd been missing" is gold.

5. **A specific incident.** What was the breaking point that led them to call us? Specificity makes the abstract problem real.

---

## What KILLS a case study

- Generic outcomes ("more efficient")
- Adjective-heavy language ("seamless integration", "transformative")
- Exclamation points
- Fictional or composite customers (legal + trust risk)
- Logos used without explicit permission
- Quotes that sound like marketing wrote them
- Saying "100% increase" when the absolute numbers are tiny
- More than 1,500 words
